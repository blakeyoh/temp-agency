# Frameworks

This pack grounds the Gen Z expert in rigorous generational method, smartphone-era youth research, phone-based-childhood debates, social information behavior, and current teen platform data. The specialist should make platform culture legible without collapsing young people into stereotypes.

## Generational-Method Caution (Pew Research Center)

- **Core claim**: Generational labels are useful only when they explain historically comparable cohort differences; otherwise they easily become marketing mythology or stereotypes.
- **Use it when**: A plan says "Gen Z wants..." without controlling for age, life stage, demographics, platform access, or period effects.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A reason to avoid all cohort analysis. Use the label as a disciplined hypothesis, not a personality type.
- **Quote snippet**: "there is great diversity of thought, experience and behavior within generations" - Pew Research Center, "How Pew Research Center will report on generations moving forward" (https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/05/22/how-pew-research-center-will-report-on-generations-moving-forward/)
- **Quote snippet**: "researchers need to look at each generation or age cohort at a similar point in the life cycle" - Pew Research Center, same source (https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/05/22/how-pew-research-center-will-report-on-generations-moving-forward/)

## Smartphone-Cohort Analysis (Jean Twenge / iGen)

- **Core claim**: The cohort born after the mid-1990s entered adolescence with smartphones, social media, and constant internet access as background conditions, changing time use, social interaction, safety orientation, and mental health debates.
- **Use it when**: A product, school, employer, or campaign assumes desktop-era attention, in-person-first socialization, or slow institutional trust.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A monocausal claim that phones explain every youth behavior or that all young users are damaged by technology.
- **Quote snippet**: "the first generation to spend their entire adolescence in the age of the smartphone" - Jean M. Twenge, *iGen*, publisher excerpt (https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/iGen/Jean-M-Twenge/9781501152016)
- **Quote snippet**: "Any generational cutoff is arbitrary" - Jean M. Twenge, *iGen*, publisher excerpt (https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/iGen/Jean-M-Twenge/9781501152016)

## Phone-Based Childhood (Jonathan Haidt)

- **Core claim**: Haidt argues that childhood shifted from play-based to phone-based, creating collective-action problems for families, schools, platforms, and policy; the useful operational move is to examine what screen-mediated life displaces, not just what it adds.
- **Use it when**: A plan touches youth mental health, schools, social media restrictions, independence, attention, or child/teen product design.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A blanket anti-phone posture. Haidt's thesis is contested; pair it with data, context, and teen agency.
- **Quote snippet**: "play-based childhood" - Jonathan Haidt, *The Anxious Generation*, publisher description (https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)
- **Quote snippet**: "phone-based childhood" - Jonathan Haidt, *The Anxious Generation*, publisher description (https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/729231/the-anxious-generation-by-jonathan-haidt/)

## Information Sensibility (Hassoun et al., CHI 2023)

- **Core claim**: Gen Z information behavior is often social, encountered, identity-linked, and peer-interpreted rather than a clean individual search for facts.
- **Use it when**: A product depends on education, misinformation resistance, institutional trust, search behavior, social proof, or creator/influencer credibility.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A claim that accuracy does not matter. The point is that accuracy competes with belonging, relevance, identity, and source relatability.
- **Quote snippet**: "Gen Zers' informational and social needs are inseparably entangled." - Hassoun et al., "Practicing Information Sensibility" (https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.07184)
- **Quote snippet**: "information journeys often do not begin with a truth-seeking search query" - Hassoun et al., same source (https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.07184)

## Current Platform and AI Baselines (Pew Research Center)

- **Core claim**: Youth platform behavior is not static: YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, AI chatbots, and group-chat-adjacent tools occupy different roles, and usage varies sharply by age, race, gender, income, and year.
- **Use it when**: A team assumes one channel reaches "young people," treats social media as a single environment, or ignores teen AI chatbot use.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A substitute for research with the actual audience. National teen data should set priors, not replace user discovery.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Pew's 2025 teen report finds daily and "almost constant" use varies by platform and demographic group, and Pew's 2026 AI report cautions that teen AI experiences vary by race, ethnicity, gender, and household income (https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/12/09/teens-social-media-and-ai-chatbots-2025/; https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2026/02/24/how-teens-use-and-view-ai/)
