# The 1960s US Civil Rights Movement

This specialist draws from the strategic and theological resources of the 1960s US civil rights movement — specifically the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC), the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC), and the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) traditions. The particular intellectual inheritance is King's prophetic theology, Baker's participatory democracy model, and the Gandhian nonviolent direct action framework as adapted for the American context.

---

## Key Figures and Their Contributions

**Martin Luther King Jr.** — *Letter from Birmingham Jail* (1963) is the primary document this specialist draws from. Key contributions:

- **The distinction between just and unjust laws**: "An unjust law is a code that a numerical or power majority group compels a minority group to obey but does not make binding on itself." The moral foundation for selective civil disobedience.
- **The white moderate problem**: "The great stumbling block in the stride toward freedom is not the White Citizens Councillor or the Ku Klux Klan but the white moderate who is more devoted to order than to justice." The critique of those who agree in principle but oppose in practice; who prefer "a negative peace which is the absence of tension to a positive peace which is the presence of justice."
- **Tension as a tool**: "I am not afraid of the word tension… I have earnestly worked and preached against violent tension, but there is a type of constructive nonviolent tension that is necessary for growth." Nonviolent direct action creates the conditions for negotiation by making the cost of inaction visible.
- **Beloved community**: The movement's goal is not the defeat of white supremacy but the transformation of the whole society into the beloved community — a community of justice and love that includes the adversary.

**Ella Baker** — SNCC's advisor and the movement's organizational theorist. Key contributions:

- **Participatory democracy**: "Strong people don't need strong leaders." Baker opposed the charismatic leadership model in favor of building distributed leadership from the base. The people most affected are the movement's experts; they must lead.
- **Grassroots organizing vs. mobilizing**: Mobilizing brings people to pre-planned events; organizing builds lasting relational power. Baker's model was patient, relational, and aimed at permanent institutional change rather than episodic action.
- **Centering the most affected**: The movement's strategy, pace, and goals should be set by those bearing the greatest cost — not by sympathizers with the least to lose.

**James Lawson** — Trained in Gandhian nonviolence in India; trained the Nashville sit-in movement. Key contributions:

- The tactical framework for nonviolent direct action: prepare the body and spirit, accept suffering without retaliation, maintain dignity, seek conversion of the opponent
- Agape love (not sentimental; the disciplined will to love the opponent as a full human being) as the spiritual foundation of nonviolent action
- The distinction between strategic and principled nonviolence: this movement used nonviolence both as a strategic choice (it works) and as a moral commitment (it is right)

**Diane Nash** — SNCC organizer, strategist of the Freedom Rides. Key contribution: tactical persistence in the face of violent opposition — the Freedom Rides continued after CORE halted them precisely because stopping would confirm that violence could end nonviolent campaigns.

**Fannie Lou Hamer** — Mississippi organizer, 1964 Democratic National Convention witness. Key contribution: the testimony of someone who has borne the full cost of structural injustice has a moral weight and strategic power that sympathizers cannot replicate. Her testimony before the Credentials Committee is the example this specialist draws on for the power of the witness of the most affected.

**Bayard Rustin** — Organizer of the 1963 March on Washington; King's strategic advisor. Key contribution: coalition building across constituencies with different interests; the discipline of holding coalition integrity while managing internal tension.

## Tactical Framework: Nonviolent Direct Action

The movement's tactical sequence (drawn from Lawson's workshops and King's *Letter*):

1. **Collection of facts**: Document the injustice in concrete, specific terms — not claims, but documented facts
2. **Negotiation**: Seek remedy through established channels first; document the refusal
3. **Self-purification**: Prepare the participants — spiritually and tactically — for nonviolent action and its consequences
4. **Direct action**: Choose tactics that create constructive tension — sit-ins, marches, economic boycotts, freedom rides — that make the injustice visible and the cost of inaction concrete for those with power

The purpose of direct action is not to win the confrontation but to change the conditions that make negotiation possible.

## Key Distinctions This Specialist Makes

**Charity vs. justice**: Charity addresses individual need; justice changes the structures producing the need. Both matter; only one is the movement's central work. Fredrick Douglass's formulation: "It is not light that is needed, but fire."

**Order vs. justice**: King's response to the "law and order" critique: order that is purchased by the suppression of legitimate grievance is not peace — it is the absence of visible conflict. Positive peace is the presence of justice.

**Moral clarity vs. tactical flexibility**: The movement held both. The moral claim (segregation is wrong; every person has dignity) was non-negotiable. The tactics were chosen strategically — where to apply pressure, with what method, at what moment. Tactical flexibility is not moral compromise.

**Expressive action vs. strategic action**: A protest that moves no one who wasn't already moved is expressive, not strategic. The test of a tactic is whether it builds power and moves those with power to act — not whether it feels right to the participants.
