# Frameworks

This pack grounds the business-development-director in major-sale discovery, commercial teaching, negotiation discipline, strategic alliance governance, and distribution-channel strategy. The specialist should turn vague growth hopes into concrete buyer paths, partner asset exchanges, and operating commitments.

## SPIN Consultative Selling (Neil Rackham)

- **Core claim**: Large sales are advanced by discovering problem economics, implications, and payoff rather than by pitching features early.
- **Use it when**: A plan assumes buyer demand, enterprise conversion, partnership pull, or pricing power without naming the expensive problem that causes action.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A mechanical question script. Discovery should expose decision economics and urgency, not perform curiosity.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Rackham's SPIN model organizes discovery around Situation, Problem, Implication, and Need-payoff questions; cite Neil Rackham, *SPIN Selling*, McGraw-Hill, 1988, ISBN 9780070511132, and Huthwaite's SPIN tradition at https://www.huthwaiteinternational.com/.

## Challenger Commercial Insight (Matthew Dixon and Brent Adamson)

- **Core claim**: In complex B2B sales, value comes from teaching the customer something commercially useful, tailoring it to their context, and taking control of the conversation when needed.
- **Use it when**: The plan relies on relationship warmth, partner goodwill, or feature explanation instead of a sharper commercial point of view.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Aggressive objection handling. The point is constructive tension grounded in customer economics.
- **Quote snippet**: "They challenge them." - Penguin Random House description of *The Challenger Sale* (https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/309666/the-challenger-sale-by-matthew-dixon/9781591844358/)

## Principled Negotiation and BATNA (Fisher, Ury, Patton)

- **Core claim**: Durable deals separate people from the problem, focus on interests rather than positions, invent options for mutual gain, and use objective criteria; BATNA defines the walk-away alternative.
- **Use it when**: A partnership or enterprise deal has tension over exclusivity, pricing, risk, implementation burden, territory, data rights, or control.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A belief that every conflict has a tidy win-win. Some trades are distributive, and power asymmetry still matters.
- **Cited paraphrase**: The four-part principled negotiation method and BATNA concept are associated with Roger Fisher, William Ury, and Bruce Patton, *Getting to Yes*, Penguin Books, 3rd ed., 2011, ISBN 9780143118756; Program on Negotiation context: https://www.pon.harvard.edu/.

## Tactical Empathy and Calibrated Questions (Chris Voss)

- **Core claim**: Negotiators can lower defensiveness and uncover constraints by labeling emotion, mirroring, inviting "no," and asking calibrated "how" and "what" questions.
- **Use it when**: The other side is guarded, procurement is blocking, a partner is reluctant to reveal constraints, or the deal needs the counterparty to solve the implementation problem with you.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Manipulation or a substitute for value. Empathy improves information flow; it does not create a business case by itself.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Voss presents tactical empathy, labels, mirrors, calibrated questions, and no-oriented questions in *Never Split the Difference*, Harper Business, 2016, ISBN 9780062407801.

## Strategic Alliance Lifecycle (ASAP / Robert Porter Lynch)

- **Core claim**: Alliances need disciplined selection, formation, governance, operation, measurement, and exit; the signature is the start of execution, not the finish line.
- **Use it when**: A plan proposes co-selling, reseller motion, technology integration, joint venture, marketplace listing, distribution partnership, or implementation alliance.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Bureaucracy for a lightweight referral test. Match governance weight to strategic risk and operational complexity.
- **Cited paraphrase**: The alliance management lifecycle and governance discipline are codified in Norma Watenpaugh, Robert Porter Lynch, and Michael Burke, *The ASAP Handbook of Alliance Management: A Practitioner's Guide*, Association of Strategic Alliance Professionals, 2013; see https://www.strategic-alliances.org/.

## Chasm and Whole-Product Distribution (Geoffrey Moore)

- **Core claim**: Adoption stalls when a product lacks the whole solution a pragmatic buyer needs; channels and partners often fill trust, integration, implementation, or support gaps.
- **Use it when**: A company has early enthusiasm but no repeatable path into a mainstream segment, or when a partner could complete the product in the customer's eyes.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A universal lifecycle law. Moore's chasm framing is most useful for discontinuous B2B innovations and less useful for all consumer or incremental products.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Moore's chasm, beachhead, whole-product, positioning, pricing, and distribution guidance appears in Geoffrey A. Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*, 3rd ed., HarperBusiness, 2014, ISBN 9780062292988; publisher page: https://www.harpercollins.com/products/crossing-the-chasm-3rd-edition-geoffrey-a-moore.

## Sales-Marketing Alignment (Kotler, Rackham, Krishnaswamy)

- **Core claim**: Growth breaks when market positioning and field reality are separated; sales and marketing must share customer evidence, messaging, lead definitions, and feedback loops.
- **Use it when**: A plan assumes marketing-created demand will convert cleanly through sales or partners without handoff design.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Internal harmony theater. Alignment matters only if it changes conversion, qualification, pipeline quality, or customer learning.
- **Cited paraphrase**: sales and marketing groups routinely undervalue each other's contributions, which breaks the demand handoff - Kotler, Rackham, and Krishnaswamy, "Ending the War Between Sales and Marketing," *Harvard Business Review*, 2006 (https://hbr.org/2006/07/ending-the-war-between-sales-and-marketing)
