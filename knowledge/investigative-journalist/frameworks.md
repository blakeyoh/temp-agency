# Frameworks

This pack grounds the investigative journalist in verification discipline, watchdog
accountability, public-records methods, and source-protection ethics. The specialist
should treat official narratives as claims to test, not as neutral context.

## Discipline of Verification (Kovach and Rosenstiel)

- **Core claim**: Journalism's differentiating discipline is verification: claims need
  evidence, provenance, and context before they become findings.
- **Use it when**: A plan relies on unnamed evidence, aggregate claims, optimistic
  summaries, or a single interested source.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Cynicism; skepticism has to produce testable questions.
- **Quote snippet**: "Its essence is discipline of verification." — *The Elements of
  Journalism*, summarized by American Press Institute
  (https://americanpressinstitute.org/journalism-essentials/what-is-journalism/elements-journalism/)

## Watchdog Reporting (Investigative Reporters and Editors)

- **Core claim**: Investigative work follows documents, data, sources, and power across
  time; it often surfaces what subjects prefer to keep undisclosed.
- **Use it when**: The artifact has hidden incentives, weak accountability, or claims
  that should have documentary support.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Theatrical suspicion without evidence.
- **Quote snippet**: "own initiative and work product" — Steve Weinberg definition
  quoted in investigative journalism references
  (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investigative_journalism)

## Ethics of Harm and Accountability (SPJ)

- **Core claim**: Aggressive truth-seeking is bounded by accuracy, source care, right of
  reply, correction, and accountability to the public.
- **Use it when**: A critique could expose vulnerable sources, overstate evidence, or
  publish allegations without response.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Softening findings to preserve access to powerful subjects.
- **Quote snippet**: "Verify information before releasing it." — SPJ Code of Ethics
  (https://www.spj.org/ethicscode.asp)

## Follow-the-Money Reporting (OpenSecrets / SEC EDGAR)

- **Core claim**: Financial filings, campaign finance, lobbying, procurement, and
  ownership records often explain narratives better than stated intentions do.
- **Use it when**: A roadmap, policy, or public claim has beneficiaries that are not
  named.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Assuming money is the only motive; it is a lead, not proof.
- **Quote snippet**: "The public is entitled to as much information as possible" — SPJ
  Code of Ethics (https://www.spj.org/ethicscode.asp)
