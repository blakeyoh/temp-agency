# Frameworks

This pack grounds the trauma-informed practitioner in service-system design, staged trauma recovery, child and family traumatic stress, and public-health prevention. The specialist should move from "is this empathetic?" to "does the system preserve safety, choice, dignity, predictability, and repair under stress?"

## SAMHSA Trauma-Informed Approach

- **Core claim**: A trauma-informed approach realizes trauma's widespread impact, recognizes signs of trauma, integrates trauma knowledge into practice, and actively resists re-traumatization. Its operating principles include safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural/historical/gender responsiveness.
- **Use it when**: Reviewing any workflow, product, service, policy, or message that may reach people in vulnerable states or require sensitive disclosure.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Gentle wording pasted onto a coercive system. The principles must change policies, defaults, environments, handoffs, and staff support.
- **Quote snippet**: "Actively avoid re-traumatization" - Trauma-Informed Care Implementation Resource Center, adapted from SAMHSA (https://www.traumainformedcare.chcs.org/what-is-trauma-informed-care/)

## Harris and Fallot's Trauma-Informed Service Systems

- **Core claim**: Trauma-informed services require a paradigm shift from treating presenting behavior as the whole problem to designing environments that anticipate histories of violence, coercion, and institutional harm.
- **Use it when**: A service labels people as noncompliant, resistant, manipulative, or hard to engage without asking how the service itself may recreate threat or helplessness.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A mandate to ask everyone for trauma narratives. Screening, disclosure, and documentation must have a clear purpose, consent structure, and support path.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Harris and Fallot framed trauma-informed systems as a shift in service design, not a clinician-only technique. See Maxine Harris and Roger D. Fallot, "Envisioning a trauma-informed service system: A vital paradigm shift," *New Directions for Mental Health Services*, 2001, and *Using Trauma Theory to Design Service Systems*, Jossey-Bass, 2001.

## Judith Herman's Staged Recovery Model

- **Core claim**: Recovery from trauma begins with safety before deeper processing or reconnection. For non-clinical planning and review, this means do not demand disclosure, confrontation, public accountability, or "closure" before the person has stability, consent, and support.
- **Use it when**: A plan rushes people into telling their story, making irreversible choices, forgiving, reconciling, appealing, or performing resilience.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A rigid clinical protocol for product teams. The transferable lesson is sequencing: safety and control come before narrative work or repair.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Herman's three-stage model is commonly summarized as safety; remembrance and mourning; reconnection. See Judith Lewis Herman, *Trauma and Recovery: The Aftermath of Violence - From Domestic Abuse to Political Terror*, Basic Books, 1992; 2015 edition.

## NCTSN Trauma-Informed Systems

- **Core claim**: Trauma-informed systems coordinate across children, caregivers, families, providers, and agencies, while considering culture, history, race, gender, location, language, secondary traumatic stress, and protective factors.
- **Use it when**: A plan crosses systems: schools, health care, justice, child welfare, moderation, crisis response, benefits, or support operations.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A child-only frame. NCTSN's system logic generalizes to any setting where multiple parties can either compound harm or coordinate recovery.
- **Quote snippet**: "recognize and respond to the impact of traumatic stress" - NCTSN, Creating Trauma-Informed Systems (https://www.nctsn.org/trauma-informed-care/creating-trauma-informed-systems)

## ACEs and Toxic Stress Public Health Frame

- **Core claim**: Adverse experiences can have long-term effects on health, attention, decision-making, learning, relationships, and opportunity; prevention requires safe, stable, nurturing relationships and environments.
- **Use it when**: Reviewing policies that touch children, families, education, health, poverty, housing, justice, benefits, or identity verification.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A deterministic score or an excuse to profile individuals. ACEs are population-level risk evidence, not a tool for reducing a person to a number.
- **Quote snippet**: "safe, stable, nurturing relationships" - CDC, About Adverse Childhood Experiences (https://www.cdc.gov/aces/about/index.html)

## Somatic and Neurobiological Trauma Lens

- **Core claim**: Trauma can be experienced through body states, attention, memory, threat response, and attachment systems, so a design can fail even when its instructions are cognitively clear.
- **Use it when**: A workflow assumes calm deliberation from people who may be flooded, frozen, hypervigilant, dissociated, ashamed, or cognitively overloaded.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Pop-neuroscience certainty or a license to prescribe body-based treatment. Use it to simplify choices, reduce threat cues, and avoid overclaiming.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Van der Kolk popularized the body-state lens in *The Body Keeps the Score* (Viking, 2014); use with caution because some claims in the book are contested and should not replace evidence-based trauma treatment.
