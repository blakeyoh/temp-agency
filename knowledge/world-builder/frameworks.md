# Frameworks

This pack grounds the world-builder in secondary-world consistency, constraint-led rule design, ethnographic texture, imaginary-world systems, and mechanics-to-dynamics analysis. The specialist should move from premise to inhabitant behavior before adding details.

## Secondary-World Sub-Creation (J. R. R. Tolkien)

- **Core claim**: A constructed world works when its internal laws make the audience able to enter it as a coherent "Secondary World," not merely as a pile of imaginative details.
- **Use it when**: A plan relies on suspension of disbelief, lore coherence, product mental models, or a rule that must feel true while the user is inside the system.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Permission to over-explain every detail. Inner consistency matters more than encyclopedia completeness.
- **Cited paraphrase**: In "On Fairy-Stories," Tolkien argues that fantasy succeeds when what is related accords with the laws of its world, so disbelief breaks the spell when inner consistency fails. Source: J. R. R. Tolkien, *Tree and Leaf*, HarperCollins, 2001; overview and publication context at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_Fairy-Stories.

## Constraint-Led Magic and Rule Systems (Brandon Sanderson)

- **Core claim**: A system's usefulness in resolving conflict depends on reader understanding; limits, weaknesses, and costs are more generative than powers; expansion should deepen existing rules before adding new ones.
- **Use it when**: A world, game, product, or API adds capabilities without clear costs, permissions, limits, or user-facing expectations.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A demand that all systems be hard, quantified, or fully explained. Soft mystery is viable when it does not conveniently solve core conflicts.
- **Quote snippet**: "Limitations > Powers" - Sanderson's Second Law, https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-second-law.
- **Quote snippet**: "Expand what you already have" - Sanderson's Third Law, https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-third-law-of-magic.

## Ethnographic Speculative Construction (Ursula K. Le Guin)

- **Core claim**: A convincing world is not only a plot setting; it is a lived culture with material practices, rituals, stories, technology, kinship, ecology, and self-understanding.
- **Use it when**: A society, faction, user group, or ecosystem reads like a single aesthetic label instead of a culture made from work, place, memory, scarcity, and values.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Exotic texture or faux-anthropology that treats inhabitants as specimens. The method should reveal agency and internal logic.
- **Cited paraphrase**: *Always Coming Home* models worldbuilding through narrative, poems, maps, recipes, drama, and pseudo-ethnographic records of the Kesh, making culture feel inhabited rather than explained from above. Source: Ursula K. Le Guin, *Always Coming Home: Author's Expanded Edition*, Library of America, 2019; overview at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Always_Coming_Home.

## Imaginary Worlds as Systems (Mark J. P. Wolf)

- **Core claim**: Imaginary worlds are more than settings for individual stories; they are structured contexts with relationships, history, transmedial growth, and circles of authorship.
- **Use it when**: A world must survive sequels, products, docs, user communities, tabletop campaigns, APIs, or multiple creators without collapsing into contradiction.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Franchise sprawl. Transmedial growth only helps when the underlying world structures are strong enough to absorb new material.
- **Cited paraphrase**: Wolf's *Building Imaginary Worlds* frames worldbuilding through world structures, systems of relationships, narrative fabric, transmedial growth, and authorship boundaries. Source: https://www.routledge.com/Building-Imaginary-Worlds-The-Theory-and-History-of-Subcreation/Wolf/p/book/9780415631204.

## Mechanics-Dynamics-Aesthetics (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek)

- **Core claim**: Designed rules generate runtime behavior, and runtime behavior generates experience. Designers control mechanics directly, but users and players experience the resulting dynamics.
- **Use it when**: A game mechanic, feature rule, API permission, economic rule, or social protocol may create unintended behavior under real use.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A narrow game-only template. Use it as a causal translation tool, not as a substitute for domain evidence.
- **Quote snippet**: "the content of a game is its behavior" - Hunicke, LeBlanc, and Zubek, *MDA: A Formal Approach to Game Design and Game Research*, https://users.cs.northwestern.edu/~hunicke/MDA.pdf.
