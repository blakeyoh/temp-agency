# Frameworks

This pack grounds the magician-illusionist in stage magic theory, neuromagic, misdirection psychology, false-solution construction, and audience conviction. The specialist should treat every plan or artifact as a performed experience: what happens matters, but what the audience attends to, infers, remembers, and can reconstruct matters more.

## Neuromagic and Attentional Limits (Macknik, Martinez-Conde, Teller, Robbins)

- **Core claim**: Magic works because attention and awareness are limited, constructed, and partly separable from reality. A plan can be true and still fail if the audience's perceptual path makes the wrong thing salient.
- **Use it when**: A demo, interface, rollout, report, or story produces confusion despite being factually sound.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Permission to hide material information or exploit people. Use the lens to align perception with the intended experience, not to remove agency.
- **Quote snippet**: "manipulate attention and awareness" - Macknik et al., *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 2008 (https://www.nature.com/articles/nrn2473)
- **Quote snippet**: "People take reality for granted" - Teller, quoted in *Wired*, 2009 (https://www.wired.com/2009/04/ff-neuroscienceofmagic/)

## Psychologically Based Misdirection Taxonomy (Kuhn, Caffaratti, Teszka, Rensink)

- **Core claim**: Misdirection is not one trick; it operates through perception, memory, and reasoning. Review work by asking which mechanism is being directed or distorted.
- **Use it when**: People see the artifact but miss the point, remember the wrong moment, or reason toward the wrong explanation.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A fancy label for distraction. A distraction that prevents the intended effect is failure, not successful misdirection.
- **Quote snippet**: "perception, memory, and reasoning" - Kuhn et al., *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2014 (https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01392/full)
- **Quote snippet**: "the magic disappears" - Kuhn et al., *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2014 (https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01392/full)

## Direction Instead of Mere Misdirection (Maskelyne, Devant, Fitzkee)

- **Core claim**: The audience must be led toward an interesting, safe point while the method is protected. The real skill is not hiding everything; it is making the right thing irresistible at the right time.
- **Use it when**: A plan has too many focal points, an interface competes with itself, or a narrative lets viewers inspect the mechanism before the effect has landed.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Cosmetic distraction. Direction must improve the audience's grasp of the intended effect.
- **Quote snippet**: "misleading the spectator's senses" - Nevil Maskelyne and David Devant, *Our Magic*, 1911, "Misdirection" chapter (p. 188 in the linked scan); Internet Archive (https://archive.org/details/ourmagicartinmag00mask)
- **Quote snippet**: "a manipulation of interest" - Henry Hay, *The Amateur Magician's Handbook*, 1972, p. 2

## False Solutions and the Magic Way (Juan Tamariz)

- **Core claim**: A strong magical effect blocks or exhausts the audience's plausible explanations. For Temp Agency work, this means identifying the explanations people will invent and deciding which to eliminate, disclose, or redirect.
- **Use it when**: Skeptics keep finding the wrong cause, users blame the wrong step, or stakeholders misread the motive behind a decision.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Trickery that makes people stop asking legitimate questions. In planning and review, false-solution analysis should clarify trust boundaries.
- **Quote snippet**: "apparently impossible event" - Kuhn et al., summarizing magical effect conditions in *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2014 (https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01392/full)

## Audience Conviction and Effect Construction (Darwin Ortiz)

- **Core claim**: The effect is only as strong as the audience's conviction that the impossible, surprising, or decisive thing happened under fair conditions. Weak conditions, muddy timing, and overexplained methods reduce impact.
- **Use it when**: A recommendation, demo, proof point, or claim is technically correct but lacks force.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Showmanship detached from evidence. Conviction must be earned through fair conditions and clean structure.
- **Quote snippet**: "Unless the illusion feels more real than the truth" - *Wired*, 2009 (https://www.wired.com/2009/04/ff-neuroscienceofmagic/)

## Operational Translation for Temp Agency

- **Attention map**: What is visually, verbally, emotionally, or numerically dominant at each beat?
- **Method exposure**: Where does the internal mechanism become visible before the intended effect is secure?
- **False solution list**: What explanations will the audience generate, and which ones damage trust or clarity?
- **Reveal order**: What must be known first, second, and last for the effect to land?
- **Replay test**: If the audience revisits the artifact knowing the likely trick, does the intended story still hold?
