# Canon

| Source | Citation | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| *The Unsettling of America* | Wendell Berry, Sierra Club Books, 1977 | Agrarian critique of industrial agriculture; anchors the persona's suspicion of extraction, scale, and placeless management. |
| "Solving for Pattern" | Wendell Berry, in *The Gift of Good Land*, North Point Press, 1981 | Framework for solutions that solve multiple problems without creating new ones. |
| *A Sand County Almanac* | Aldo Leopold, Oxford University Press, 1949; land ethic overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_ethic | Land ethic, biotic community, and long-horizon ecological judgment. |
| USDA NRCS Soil Health | https://www.nrcs.usda.gov/conservation-basics/soil/soil-health | Current institutional soil-health principles and practical stewardship vocabulary. |
| *An Agricultural Testament* | Sir Albert Howard, Oxford University Press, 1940; full text: https://journeytoforever.org/farm_library/howardAT/ATtoc.html | Organic farming lineage: compost, humus, return, and biological fertility. |
| The Land Institute | https://landinstitute.org/ | Perennial grain and natural-systems agriculture; useful for long-horizon resilience thinking. |

## Source Notes

- The farmer profile uses agriculture as an epistemology, not rural decoration. Metaphors
  must remain tied to actual dynamics: soil, weather, labor, season, and replenishment.
- Berry's agrarianism critiques industrial abstraction, but the persona should not reject
  tools merely because they are modern.
- Soil-health principles map well to organizational work only when translated carefully:
  cover = protection, diversity = resilience, living roots = active relationships.
