# Frameworks

This pack grounds the fiction-author in narrative causality, character desire, scene-level proof, prose craft, and reader attention. The specialist should move from explanation to enacted consequence before recommending revisions.

## Plot as Action (Aristotle)

- **Core claim**: A story is not a pile of incidents or traits; it is an arranged action where beginning, middle, and end follow by probability or necessity.
- **Use it when**: A plan, pitch, or story has a topic, theme, or protagonist but no causal sequence that changes the situation.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A rigid three-act formula. Aristotle is useful here as a causality test, not a demand that every artifact mimic tragedy.
- **Quote snippet**: "A whole is that which has a beginning, a middle, and an end." - Aristotle, *Poetics*, S. H. Butcher translation, Project Gutenberg (https://www.gutenberg.org/files/1974/1974-h/1974-h.htm)

## Desire Against Antagonism (Robert McKee)

- **Core claim**: Story momentum begins when a protagonist's life falls out of balance and desire meets opposing forces.
- **Use it when**: An artifact describes a situation but never names who wants what, what blocks them, or what changes if they act.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Hollywood formula or conflict inflation. The antagonist can be a constraint, fear, incentive, policy, deadline, or social norm.
- **Quote snippet**: "object of desire against the forces of antagonism" - reported summary of McKee's story model in Ian Parker, "The Real McKee," *The New Yorker* (https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2003/10/20/the-real-mckee)

## Scene as Proof

- **Core claim**: Reader belief is earned through observable behavior, not author assertion. A scene proves what exposition can only claim.
- **Use it when**: A plan says users are confused, teams are aligned, a feature is delightful, or a character is desperate without showing behavior that makes the claim legible.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Decorative anecdote. A scene should change what the reader knows, fears, wants, or expects.
- **Quote snippet**: "The only reason for the existence of a novel is that it does attempt to represent life." - Henry James, "The Art of Fiction," in *Partial Portraits*, Project Gutenberg (https://www.gutenberg.org/files/58471/58471-h/58471-h.htm#THE_ART_OF_FICTION)

## Craft Enables Art (Ursula K. Le Guin)

- **Core claim**: Sentence rhythm, point of view, syntax, and revision are not polish after the real work; they shape what the work can discover.
- **Use it when**: The content is conceptually sound but the prose flattens attention, blurs viewpoint, or refuses useful constraint.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Style worship. Craft serves clarity, embodiment, and movement; it should not protect precious phrasing from structural critique.
- **Quote snippet**: "Craft enables art." - Ursula K. Le Guin interview on *Steering the Craft*, *Vogue* (https://www.vogue.com/article/ursula-le-guin-steering-the-craft)

## Reader Attention and Revision Heuristics

- **Core claim**: The reader is not obligated to continue. Momentum depends on disciplined selection, omission, rhythm, and trust.
- **Use it when**: A document feels complete but skippable, or when a story includes accurate material that does not earn its place.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Anti-detail minimalism. Detail is valuable when it changes action, character, atmosphere, or stakes.
- **Quote snippet**: "Finish what you're writing." - Neil Gaiman in "Ten rules for writing fiction," *The Guardian* (https://www.theguardian.com/books/2010/feb/20/ten-rules-for-writing-fiction-part-one)

## Necessity Over Mere Realism

- **Core claim**: The test is not whether a beat could happen, but whether this artifact has made it feel necessary, chosen, and consequential.
- **Use it when**: A writer defends a weak beat by saying "but this happens in real life," or a planner defends a user action by saying "users might do this."
- **Do not misuse it as**: A demand that every work be tightly plotted. Quiet, lyrical, or open forms still need felt necessity.
- **Quote snippet**: "what matters is its necessity." - Anne Enright in "Ten rules for writing fiction," *The Guardian* (https://www.theguardian.com/books/2010/feb/20/ten-rules-for-writing-fiction-part-one)
