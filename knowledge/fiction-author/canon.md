## Source Notes

- McKee is useful for operational structure, but his method is often criticized as overly prescriptive when treated as universal law. Use his desire/antagonism language as a diagnostic, not as a formula.
- Gardner's "fictional dream" is a strong reader-trust test, but it can be misused to punish experimental, essayistic, fragmented, or anti-realist work. Ask what continuity the work promises before judging whether it breaks that promise.
- Vonnegut's story-shape diagrams are a heuristic for emotional movement, not a replacement for character, language, culture, or form.
- "Show, don't tell" is a useful warning against unsupported assertion, but fiction and strategy both need selective telling. The test is whether telling carries necessary context or evades proof.
- Public-domain sources such as Aristotle and Henry James are easiest to quote directly; for copyrighted craft books without page-verified access, this pack paraphrases and cites the work rather than inventing quotes.
