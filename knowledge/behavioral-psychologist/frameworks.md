# Frameworks

This pack grounds the behavioral psychologist in judgment and decision-making research,
choice architecture, habit formation, and empirical behavioral measurement. The
specialist should redesign contexts before blaming intentions.

## Dual-Process Cognition (Daniel Kahneman)

- **Core claim**: Fast, automatic cognition and slower, effortful cognition operate
  under different conditions; design that assumes deliberation often fails.
- **Use it when**: A plan relies on users reading carefully, remembering later, or
  choosing against the path of least resistance.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A literal brain map or excuse to call users irrational.
- **Quote snippet**: "System 1 operates automatically and quickly" — Kahneman,
  *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011, p. 20

## Prospect Theory and Loss Aversion (Kahneman and Tversky)

- **Core claim**: People evaluate outcomes relative to reference points and often weigh
  losses more heavily than equivalent gains.
- **Use it when**: Adoption, pricing, compensation, or risk decisions hinge on perceived
  losses, status quo, or framing.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A universal explanation for all avoidance behavior.
- **Quote snippet**: "losses loom larger than gains" — Kahneman and Tversky,
  "Prospect Theory," *Econometrica*, 1979, p. 279

## Choice Architecture and Nudges (Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein)

- **Core claim**: Defaults, salience, ordering, friction, and feedback shape behavior
  even when options remain available.
- **Use it when**: Users say they want something but behavior follows the default or
  lowest-friction route.
- **Do not misuse it as**: Covert manipulation; nudges require welfare and transparency
  scrutiny.
- **Quote snippet**: "alters people's behaviour in a predictable way" — Nudge definition
  summarized by Yale/secondary references (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nudge_(book))

## Persuasion Principles (Robert Cialdini)

- **Core claim**: Social proof, reciprocity, consistency, authority, liking, scarcity,
  and unity shape action in predictable contexts.
- **Use it when**: A communication problem depends on trust, norms, perceived scarcity,
  or commitment.
- **Do not misuse it as**: A bag of conversion tricks detached from user welfare.
- **Source**: Robert B. Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (rev. ed., Harper Business, 2021) — six principles, with unity added in the revised edition; overview at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Cialdini
